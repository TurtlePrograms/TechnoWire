1956

William Shockley

Gordon Moore, a co-founder of Intel, made significant contributions to the invention of the transistor, which ultimately earned him the Nobel Prize in Physics. He's also known for coining the term "Moore's Law," which observes that the number of transistors on a microchip tends to double roughly every two years. This exponential growth in transistor density has been a driving force behind the rapid increase in computing power. Additionally, William Shockley gained notoriety for referring to a group of eight employees who left his team as the "Traitorous Eight," a term that has become part of tech industry lore.

1975

Beginning of Silicon Valley(HP in Garage)

Silicon Valley, which emerged approximately 84 years ago in 1939, is situated in the state of California, United States, encompassing an expansive area spanning approximately 3,000 square kilometers (1,158.31 square miles). The emblematic tree that represents Silicon Valley is the towering "sequoia" or "redwood" tree. The valley owes its name to the convergence of "silicon," a vital material in the semiconductor industry, and "valley," a reference to its geographical location. This unique combination of factors has made Silicon Valley a globally renowned hub for technology and innovation.

1975

The Altair 8800 is released

TThe Altair 8800, a widely acclaimed microcomputer kit, made its debut in 1975 and is frequently regarded as one of the pioneering personal computers. This groundbreaking device played a pivotal role in shaping the early history of personal computing.

1977

Apple II is presented

Around 200 Apple I computers were manufactured, with their worth varying considerably. Some rare specimens can command prices reaching hundreds of thousands of dollars. Notably, Daniel Kottke engineered a portable version of the Apple II housed in a suitcase. The founding trio of Apple consisted of Steve Jobs, Steve Wozniak, and Ronald Wayne. Their collaboration marked the genesis of a tech empire that would shape the future of computing. The development of personal computing was significantly influenced by the hippie culture of the 1960s and 70s. Many tech pioneers, including Steve Jobs, were deeply impacted by the counterculture movement of that era. This influence led to the adoption of creative and innovative approaches in technology, contributing to the unique and transformative character of the personal computer industry.

1984

Launch of the Macintosh

The Macintosh computer stood out for its revolutionary features, notably the introduction of a graphical user interface (GUI) and the integration of a mouse. These innovations were groundbreaking for personal computers, setting the Macintosh apart and marking a significant milestone in the evolution of computing technology.

1998

Google is founded

AltaVista was a prominent search engine company during the nascent stages of the internet. However, it eventually faded into obscurity with the rise of more dominant search engines like Google. Speaking of Google, it was founded by the visionary duo of Larry Page and Sergey Brin. Notably, the very first Google server was ingeniously crafted using LEGO blocks, reflecting the company's early commitment to innovative solutions and creative problem-solving in the world of technology.
